# Abdominal & Gastrointestinal Symptom Protocol

## EMERGENCY — Route to ER
- Severe sudden abdominal pain ("worst pain ever," knife-like)
- Rigid or board-like abdomen (possible perforation or peritonitis)
- Abdominal pain + fever + vomiting (possible appendicitis or peritonitis)
- Vomiting blood (hematemesis)
- Black tarry stools (melena — possible upper GI bleed)
- Bright red blood in stool with dizziness or fainting
- Severe abdominal pain in known gallstone/kidney stone patient
- Abdominal trauma with pain

## URGENT — Same-day Gastroenterology
- Moderate abdominal pain localized to one area (right lower = possible appendicitis)
- Persistent vomiting (more than 4 episodes, unable to keep fluids down)
- Severe diarrhea with fever or blood in stool
- Jaundice (yellow skin or eyes) with abdominal pain
- Abdominal distension with pain and no bowel movements for >3 days
- Severe constipation with bloating and pain
- Suspected food poisoning with significant symptoms

## NON-URGENT — Routine Gastroenterology OPD
- Mild intermittent abdominal pain, not worsening
- Heartburn or acid reflux, chronic, stable
- Known irritable bowel syndrome, routine review
- Mild constipation without pain
- Mild diarrhea (1–3 loose stools), no blood, no fever
- Indigestion or bloating

## SELF-CARE
- Mild stomach upset after a meal
- Gas or bloating without pain
- Minor diarrhea (once or twice, no fever, no blood)

## Pain Location Guidance
- Right lower quadrant pain → Consider appendicitis → URGENT
- Right upper quadrant pain → Consider gallbladder → URGENT
- Left lower quadrant pain → Consider bowel issue
- Diffuse severe pain → EMERGENCY
- Epigastric (above navel) burning → Acid reflux/peptic ulcer → NON_URGENT unless severe

## Routing
→ Primary Department: Gastroenterology
→ Emergency Override: ER if vomiting blood, rigid abdomen, severe acute pain
→ Urgency: EMERGENCY (acute severe) / URGENT (localized + fever, vomiting) / NON_URGENT (chronic mild)
